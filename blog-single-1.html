How is Coding like playing with LEGOs?

  • Athul Harial
  • Programming
  • 14

I know it may be hard to imagine that the two can be related, especially considering that LEGOs are physical objects and coding is a written language, but there’s a lot more to it than you may think! Growing up, I remember the first time I ever touched a LEGO brick I had no idea what to do with it. I was given a huge bin full of miscellaneous pieces in all shapes, sizes, and colors. It was overwhelming when I spilled it out onto the floor, and spread the pieces around. It was an absolute mess!

Then I found the instructions — a book of ideas to follow along with. Suddenly, I was building spaceships, cars, and castles! My confidence with every little piece grew as I continued to build everything in that book. But then there was nothing else left to build. What now? Where do I even begin? How could I ever build anything half as cool as what’s in those instructions?

I think that’s the problem most beginner developers deal with: bridging the gap between beginner and intermediate. Like anyone else, I remember the first time I followed my first tutorial, and wrote my first “Hello World!” program. It was small, simple, and easy. What followed were similar tutorials that continued to build on my understanding of programming concepts and hammer home syntax, but my hand was held the entire way and I had no idea what to do with what I learned. It also didn’t help that programming was so vast that searching for answers left you more confused than it helped. So what do you do?

Build! Seriously. There’s no replacement for actual, real life experience. The quickest way to grow is to actually do it, and to make mistakes along the way. To figure it out as you go. Yeah, not everything will turn out the way you want it to, but guess what? It’s not the end of the world! Practice is what makes us better. Learning from your mistakes teaches you how to avoid the same issues in the future.

The quickest way to grow is to actually do it, and to make mistakes along the way

It’s hard to imagine how someone could build Airbnb, Instagram, or Youtube when you look at it for the first time. It’s the same feeling you get when you look at one of those gigantic LEGO city dioramas, or sculptures. They’re massive, intricate, and clearly took a lot of time, and thought. But you need to remind yourself that the way they built it was by starting small, and planning. In our case, that’s when we break out the white board: drawing out board models, relationships, user stories, and deliverables, and planning out the beginnings of a minimal viable product.t put the cart before the horse, and actually plan!
